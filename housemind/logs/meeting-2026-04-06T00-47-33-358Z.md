# Meeting Log

**Date:** 2026-04-06T00:47:33.360Z

**Topic:** BIG DECISION: Should we merge the Flask prototype (figmaTem) features into our Next.js app, or keep figmaTem as a separate tool?

WHAT WE HAVE NOW:
1. Next.js app — invite-only auth, curated product catalog, project boards, comments, feedback widget. Clean, tested, modern stack. But no visual interaction.
2. Flask prototype (figmaTem) — THE killer feature: image carousel with emoji annotation system. Users long-press on reference images, pin emoji markers, link products to those pins, browse products filtered by annotations. This is the magic that makes the app special. But it has no catalog, no invite auth, no tests, SQLite only, vanilla JS.

THREE OPTIONS:
A) MERGE into Next.js — port the annotation system, image carousel, and hierarchical projects into the Next.js app. One unified product. But adds 4 new tables, 7 new API routes, 3-4 client libraries, and ~20 new files. Big effort.
B) KEEP SEPARATE — run figmaTem as a standalone visual workspace tool. Next.js handles catalog, auth, projects. Link between them somehow. Two products to maintain.
C) REBUILD figmaTem in Next.js from scratch — don't port, redesign the annotation UX for React/mobile-first. Takes longer but cleaner result.

Each department should fight for what matters to them:
- Marketing: What sells? What do users actually want? One product or two? What is the story we tell architects and homeowners?
- Operations: What can suppliers and contractors actually use? Mobile matters. Training cost matters. Two tools = confusion?
- Tech: What is buildable in reasonable time? What about technical debt? Performance with many annotations?

Fight it out.

---

## Round 1

### Head of Marketing

Here's the cold truth: merging the Flask prototype into Next.js is the only move that makes sense for growth *long-term.* Why? A unified product means a cleaner user story—architects, contractors, and homeowners can seamlessly visualize, annotate, and purchase all in one tool. Positioning multiple disconnected products will dilute the "killer feature" and confuse users.

Separating tools fractures monetization potential and onboarding flow—you want *every user* interacting with the annotation feature, *then* diving into projects, catalogs, and comments. "Two tools" inevitably leads to weaker adoption funnels. Rebuilding in Next.js is appealing for future-proofing but delays our getting-to-market speed. Users want frictionless interaction **yesterday.**

Merging risks slowing development near-term, but the clarity of one **sharpened spear** for acquisition, retention, and monetization should drive the choice. Look at your competitors—do they split functionality across apps?

So, what’s the counterargument about scaling or user confusion if we merge? Convince me if you see one.

### Head of Operations

[Head of Operations]: Marketing's point about frictionless user flows resonates, but "merging" still introduces operational headaches. Suppliers and contractors need lightweight tools with quick onboarding—even multi-tab workflows can frustrate less tech-savvy users. If we add complexity (new features, APIs, client libraries) too fast, we risk bogging down adoption and training. Plus, field use—mobile annotations while on-site—needs rigorous testing. 

Keeping it separate (Option B) would simplify maintenance for contractors—all they care about is visualizing and agreeing on products quickly. The link between tools can be made seamless with deep links or shared projects. However, this makes Marketing's job harder, as discussing "two tools" becomes murky and less compelling. 

Challenge to Tech: What’s the learning curve if we fully merge—can users intuitively pick up the system without frustrating errors? Would separating apps help us focus and iterate faster?

### Head of Tech

[Head of Technology]: Both Marketing and Operations bring valid concerns. Merging into Next.js (Option A) provides the cleanest long-term alignment, but the tech complexity can't be overstated. We're talking about substantial feature porting—image carousel, annotation system, new data infrastructure (relational models for annotations, relationships between pins and products), and likely additional performance optimizations for high volumes of annotations. This will slow down short-term delivery.

Keeping them separate (Option B) minimizes the immediate risks, but we'd add maintenance overhead—Flask is old tech debt, and syncing two platforms (e.g., login systems, project hierarchies) can snowball into fragile connections. Rebuilding in Next.js (Option C) appeals from a "clean slate" perspective, but rebuilds *always* take longer than expected, and we'd delay delivering value to users. 

What's our MVP here—how critical is immediate annotation UX vs. de-risking our stack? Should we merge bite-sized features piecemeal into Next.js instead of all-or-nothing?
